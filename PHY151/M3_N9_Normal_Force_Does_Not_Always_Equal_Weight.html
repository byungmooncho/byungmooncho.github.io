Normal Force Does Not Always Equal Weight

Synopsis Every scale on Earth measures the normal force — how hard you press on the surface — not your true weight. The two agree only when you are not accelerating. In this note you will alter a scale's reading with your own motion, then record the normal force with a Vernier force platform while riding an elevator, and explain every feature of the graph with Newton's laws.
Performance expectations Measure normal force in inertial and accelerating frames · Draw free-body diagrams of an object in both · Describe the relationship between normal force and weight in both.

The four experiments

  1. Spring scale: hang a mass, then accelerate the scale up and down. Watch the reading at every phase — starting, moving, stopping.
  2. Digital scale, tilted: the reading drops as the angle steepens. Your free-body diagram explains why — the scale reads a force component, not a mass.
  3. Bathroom scale squats: stand still, then squat down and up. Which way does the needle deflect at each phase of the motion, and what does that tell you?
  4. The elevator ride: the Vernier force platform records normal force vs. time for a full trip. Start from the second floor and descend. Every bump and dip in that graph is Newton's second law speaking.

The key relationships

All three come from one idea: apply ΣF = ma to the object standing on the scale, with up as positive.

  • N=mg at rest or constant velocity — the only case where the scale shows true weight
  • N=m(g+a) accelerating upward (a > 0): you feel heavier; downward (a < 0): lighter
  • N=0 when a=−g free fall — apparent weightlessness, the scale reads zero
