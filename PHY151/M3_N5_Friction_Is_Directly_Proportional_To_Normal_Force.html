Friction Is Directly Proportional to the Normal Force

Synopsis Without friction, you can't walk. To move forward you push backward on the ground, and it is the ground's grip — friction — that pushes you forward in return: Newton's third law in action. That grip depends on just two things: the normal force pressing the surfaces together, and the surface roughness, μ. And note the odd geometry — friction and the normal force are directly proportional yet perpendicular to each other: the one case where a vertical force sets the size of a horizontal one.
Performance expectations Define and describe the friction force · Recognize that friction is proportional to normal force (thus weight, thus mass) and to surface roughness · Explain how a vertical force sets the size of a horizontal one, and why μ = 0 on a perfectly smooth surface means no friction at all.

The key relationships

Two regimes, one structure: friction is a coefficient times the normal force. The inequality in the static case matters — static friction adjusts itself up to a maximum.

  • fk= μkN kinetic friction — surfaces sliding past each other
  • fs≤ μsN static friction — grips as hard as needed, up to its limit
  • μs> μk breaking the grip is harder than keeping the slide — why wheels lock and joints creak loose
